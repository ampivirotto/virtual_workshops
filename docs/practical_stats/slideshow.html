Research Question -> Analysis Plan

Identify Outcome Variable: This is the primary variable you are measuring and seeking to understand or predict. It is often referred to as the dependent variable.

Figure Out Predictor and Grouping Variables: These are the independent variables that you manipulate or observe to see their effects on the outcome variable.

Determine if Your Analysis is Independent or Paired/Repeated : This decision depends on your study design. Independent analyses involve different subjects in separate groups, whereas paired or repeated analyses involve the same subjects measured multiple times.

Decide if You Want to Test for Association, Comparison, Prediction, or Estimation: Clearly define your analysis objective(s). You might explore several of these depending on your research question.

Testing for association involves exploring relationships between variables

C omparison involves examining differences between groups

P rediction involves forecasting outcomes based on predictor variables

E stimation involves quantifying parameters within a population

Sampling Distributions

  • A sample statistic, like a mean or proportion, will vary from sample to sample.
  • Variation between sample to sample is called sampling variability.
  • A sampling distribution is the dis tribution of a statistic across many hypothetical repeated samples.

Slide 10

Review of Hypothesis Testing

  • Null Hypothesis: The default assumption; usually says there is no difference, no effect, or no association. If there is a difference, it’s due to chance alone.
  • Alternative Hypothesis: The research-based possibility; says there is a difference, an effect, or an association.
  • One-Way Test: Test for an effect in a specific direction.
  • Two-Way Test: Tests for effects in either direction.

Slide 12

Assumptions and Diagnostics

  • Parametric tests
  • Typically assume a particular form for the data or residuals
  • Often require conditions like:
  • independent observations
  • roughly normal distributions or residuals
  • similar variance across groups
  • Examples:
  • t-test, ANOVA, Pearson correlation, linear regression
  • Nonparametric tests
  • Make fewer distributional assumptions
  • Often rely on ranks rather than raw values
  • Useful when data are skewed, ordinal, or include outliers/small samples
  • Examples:
  • Mann–Whitney U test, Wilcoxon signed-rank test, Kruskal–Wallis test, Spearman correlation

Slide 13

T-Test

  • A t-test compares means.
  • Use it when:
  • The outcome is quantitative
  • You are comparing one mean or two means
  • The data are appropriate for a parametric test
  • Common versions:
  • One-sample t-test: compare one mean to a hypothesized value
  • Independent samples t-test: compare two separate groups
  • Paired t-test: compare two related measurements from the same unit

Slide 14

ANOVA

  • Used to compare mean values across 3 or more independent groups
  • Features
  • Outcome variable: quantitative
  • Grouping variable: categorical
  • Tests whether at least one group mean differs
  • Does not identify which groups differ by itself
  • Types:
  • One-Way ANOVA : Used when you have one categorical independent variable (factor) with three or more levels (groups) and one continuous dependent variable.
  • Two-Way ANOVA : Used when you have two categorical independent variables and one continuous dependent variable. It can assess the main effects of each independent variable and their interaction effect.
  • MANOVA (Multivariate Analysis of Variance) : Used when you have one or more categorical independent variables and two or more continuous dependent variables.

Slide 15

Effect Size: How Large is the Effect?

  • Effect size describes the magnitude of a difference, relationship, or association.
  • It helps us move beyond “Is it significant?” to ask “How large is the effect?”
  • A result can be:
  • statistically significant but small
  • not statistically significant but still potentially meaningful
  • Effect sizes are often more informative than p-values alone.
  • Examples:
  • Difference in Means (ANOVA)
  • Standardized Mean Difference (Cohen’s D)
  • Proportion of variance explained (Eta-squared)
  • Strength of relationship (correlation coefficient)
